Artificial Cloning of Plants

Unit: Horticulture & Biology
Introduction: While plants clone themselves naturally, humans have developed techniques to force this process. This is called Artificial Propagation. Gardeners and farmers use these methods to ensure that every new plant has the exact same fruit size, taste, and disease resistance as the parent. There are four main methods: Cuttings, Layering, Grafting, and Budding.

1. Cuttings

This is the simplest method. A piece of the parent plant (usually a stem, root, or leaf) is cut off. The cut end is often dipped in rooting hormone powder to encourage growth and then placed in moist soil. The cutting eventually develops its own roots and becomes an independent plant.

Used for: Lavender, Rosemary, Jade Plant.

2. Layering

In layering, a stem is encouraged to grow roots while still attached to the parent plant. A low-hanging branch is bent down and buried in the soil. Once roots form at the buried point, the stem is cut from the parent. Air Layering involves wrapping a branch in damp moss to grow roots in mid-air!

Used for: Climbing roses, Jasmine, Honeysuckle.

3. Grafting

Grafting joins two different plants together. The top part (the Scion) is selected for good fruit/flowers. The bottom part (the Rootstock) is selected for strong roots and disease resistance. The two are cut, joined, and taped until their tissues heal together.

Used for: Apples, Citrus trees, Avocados.

4. Budding (Shield Grafting)

Budding is a specific type of grafting. Instead of using a whole stick for the scion, a single bud is sliced from the parent tree. A "T-shaped" cut is made in the bark of the rootstock, and the single bud is slipped inside and taped shut. It is very efficient as one branch can provide many buds.

Used for: Rose bushes, Stone fruits (Peaches, Plums).

Why Go to All This Trouble?

1. Consistency: Apple seeds are genetically random. If you plant a seed from a delicious Honeycrisp apple, the tree that grows will likely produce small, sour fruit. To get Honeycrisp apples, you must graft a Honeycrisp branch onto a tree.

2. Speed: A cutting or grafted tree is already "mature." It will produce fruit in 1-3 years, whereas a tree grown from seed might take 10 years.

3. Resilience: We can graft a delicate French grape vine onto wild American roots (rootstock) that are immune to soil diseases. This gives us the best of both worlds: good fruit and tough roots.

Part A: Key Terminology

Define the following terms:

1. Scion
The top part of a graft that produces the leaves, flowers, and fruit (the desired variety).
2. Rootstock
The bottom part of a graft that provides the root system and disease resistance.
3. Rooting Hormone
A chemical powder/gel applied to the cut end of a stem to stimulate new root growth.

Part E: Scientific Analysis
9. The "Apple Seed" Problem: If you plant a seed from a Granny Smith apple, the tree that grows will not produce Granny Smith apples. Explain why.
Seeds are sexual reproduction (random DNA mix). To get the same apple, you need a clone (grafting).
10. Why is Layering considered safer for the plant than taking a Cutting?
In layering, the new plant is still attached to the parent, so it continues receiving water/nutrients while growing roots.
11. In grafting, why is it critical that the tissues of the Scion and Rootstock line up perfectly?
The internal transport tubes (xylem/phloem) must connect so water can move from the roots to the leaves.

Part G: Troubleshooting
14. Scenario: A student takes a cutting, strips all the leaves off, and puts it in dry soil. It dies. Identify two mistakes.
1. Soil must be moist. 2. Some leaves are needed for energy (though too many cause water loss).
15. Critical Thinking: Can you graft an Apple branch onto an Orange tree? Why or why not?
No. They are too genetically different (incompatible). You must graft related species (e.g., Orange on Lemon).
