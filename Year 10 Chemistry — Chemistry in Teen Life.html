L1 — Phone Battery Chemistry⚡ ★★☆

Fun fact: your phone is basically a pocket-sized chemistry set.

1. Many phone batteries are lithium-ion batteries.

2. During charging, tiny charged particles called ions move inside the battery.

3. A battery stores energy as chemical energy.

4. Overheating a battery can speed up unwanted reactions.

5. This is why damaged batteries should be recycled safely.

6. True or False: Battery chemistry is involved every time you charge a phone. True

L2 — Skincare & pH🧴 ★★☆

Chemistry link: cleansers, toners and shampoos all care about pH.

1. pH tells us how acidic or alkaline something is.

2. A pH below 7 is usually called acidic.

3. A pH above 7 is usually called alkaline.

4. Skin is naturally slightly acidic.

5. Products that are too harsh can irritate the skin's barrier.

6. True or False: pH is chemistry you can find in bathroom products. True

L3 — Energy Drinks & Electrolytes🥤 ★★☆

Sports drink labels are full of chemistry words.

1. Electrolytes are substances that form charged ions in water.

2. Sodium ions are written as Na⁺.

3. Potassium ions are written as K⁺.

4. Ions help nerve and muscle cells send signals.

5. Too much sugar in drinks means lots of dissolved molecules.

6. True or False: Dissolving salts in water can produce ions. True

L4 — Sneaker Foam & Polymers👟 ★★☆

Bouncy shoe soles are chemistry doing engineering.

1. A polymer is a long chain of repeating units.

2. Many plastics and foams are made from polymers.

3. The foam in trainers can absorb impact.

4. Stretchy materials often contain flexible molecular chains.

5. Reusing or recycling materials can reduce plastic waste.

6. True or False: Polymers can be designed to feel soft, hard, stretchy or bouncy. True

L5 — Toothpaste & Fluoride🪥 ★★☆

A small ion can make a big difference to teeth.

1. Fluoride is often written as the ion F⁻.

2. Tooth enamel contains minerals based on calcium and phosphate.

3. Acids from bacteria can attack tooth enamel.

4. Fluoride helps enamel become more resistant to acid.

5. Sugary snacks can feed mouth bacteria.

6. True or False: Dental health is linked to acid-base chemistry. True

L6 — Hair Dye & Oxidation💇 ★★☆

Colour changes in hair dye are controlled chemical reactions.

1. Oxidation is a type of chemical reaction.

2. Permanent hair dye often uses an oxidising agent.

3. A colour change can be evidence that new substances formed.

4. Hair is mostly made from the protein keratin.

5. Patch tests help reduce the risk of skin reactions.

6. True or False: A colour change always means magic, not chemistry. False

L7 — Phone Screen Glass📱 ★★☆

The smooth screen you swipe is a clever material, not “just glass”.

1. Glass often contains silicon dioxide, also called silica.

2. The formula for silicon dioxide is SiO₂.

3. SiO₂ contains silicon and oxygen.

4. Phone glass is strengthened by changing its structure.

5. A material's properties depend on its atoms and bonds.

6. True or False: Chemistry helps engineers design tougher screens. True

L8 — Snacks: Maillard Reaction🍟 ★★☆

Toast, chips and baked cookies taste good because chemistry happened.

1. The Maillard reaction helps browned food develop flavour.

2. It happens between amino acids and reducing sugars.

3. Heating food can create new aroma molecules.

4. Burning food is different because it forms lots of carbon.

5. Cooking is applied chemistry.

6. True or False: Browned food can involve chemical reactions, not just drying out. True

L9 — Sunscreen Chemistry☀️ ★★☆

Sunscreen is chemistry protecting biology.

1. Sunscreen protects skin from ultraviolet, or UV, light.

2. Some sunscreen chemicals absorb UV energy.

3. Mineral sunscreens can contain zinc oxide.

4. Zinc oxide contains zinc and oxygen.

5. Reapplying matters because sunscreen can rub or wash off.

6. True or False: Sunscreen is an everyday use of chemical properties. True

L10 — Deodorant & Smell Molecules🧼 ★★☆

Smells are molecules your nose can detect.

1. Body odour is caused by small smell molecules.

2. Bacteria on skin can break down sweat compounds.

3. Some deodorants use fragrance to mask smell.

4. Some products use acid-base chemistry to neutralise odours.

5. Antiperspirants reduce the amount of sweat released.

6. True or False: Your sense of smell is detecting chemicals. True

L11 — Clothes, Dyes & Fast Fashion👕 ★★☆

Your hoodie colour is molecular design.

1. Dyes are coloured molecules.

2. Dye molecules can bond to fabric fibres.

3. Cotton is mostly made from the natural polymer cellulose.

4. Polyester is a synthetic polymer.

5. Washing clothes can release tiny plastic fibres called microfibres.

6. True or False: Chemistry affects the colour, feel and environmental impact of clothes. True

L12 — Fireworks & Flame Colours🎆 ★★☆

No making fireworks — just decoding the chemistry of the colours.

1. Firework colours can come from heated metal ions.

2. Strontium compounds can give a red colour.

3. Barium compounds can give a green colour.

4. Copper compounds can give a blue or blue-green flame.

5. Different ions release different colours of light.

6. True or False: The same chemical always gives every firework colour. False

L13 — Fizzy Drinks & Carbon Dioxide🫧 ★★☆

That satisfying can-open sound is gas escaping solution.

1. Fizzy drinks contain dissolved carbon dioxide.

2. Carbon dioxide has the formula CO₂.

3. When pressure drops, CO₂ leaves the liquid as bubbles.

4. CO₂ in water can form a weak acid called carbonic acid.

5. Shaking a bottle creates more places for bubbles to form.

6. True or False: Fizz is related to solubility and pressure. True

L14 — Earbuds, Magnets & Elements🎧 ★★☆

Tiny earbuds use elements from the periodic table.

1. Some strong small magnets contain neodymium, symbol Nd.

2. Iron has the symbol Fe.

3. Magnets in speakers help turn electrical signals into sound.

4. Neodymium is called a rare-earth element.

5. Recycling electronics can help recover useful metals.

6. True or False: The periodic table appears inside modern tech. True

L15 — Blue Light, LEDs & Semiconductors💡 ★★☆

Screen colours depend on carefully chosen materials.

1. LEDs are made from semiconductor materials.

2. Gallium nitride can be used in blue LEDs.

3. Gallium has the symbol Ga.

4. Nitrogen has the symbol N.

5. Different materials can release different colours of light.

6. True or False: Chemistry helps explain why screens can glow in colour. True
