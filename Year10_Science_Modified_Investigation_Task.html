📘 Background Information

Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins produced by living cells that speed up chemical reactions without being permanently consumed or altered. Each enzyme has a uniquely shaped region called the active site, which binds specifically to its target molecule (the substrate). This is described by the "Lock and Key" model: just as a specific key fits a specific lock, only the correct substrate fits an enzyme's active site.

Catalase is an enzyme found in nearly all living organisms, including potato cells. It catalyses the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) — a toxic waste product of cellular metabolism — into harmless water (H₂O) and oxygen gas (O₂). The oxygen produced is the bubbling you often see when hydrogen peroxide contacts a cut or a piece of liver/potato.

Several factors affect how efficiently an enzyme works, including temperature, pH, and substrate concentration. There is a specific optimum temperature at which each enzyme functions most efficiently. Below this temperature, enzyme and substrate molecules move slowly (less kinetic energy), reducing the frequency of successful collisions. Above the optimum temperature, the enzyme denatures — the high thermal energy breaks the bonds that maintain the enzyme's shape, permanently distorting the active site so the substrate can no longer bind. Denaturation is irreversible.

🎯 Aim & Hypothesis

Aim: To investigate the effect of temperature on the rate of catalase enzyme activity by measuring the volume of oxygen gas produced when potato discs are added to hydrogen peroxide solution at eight different temperatures over 60 seconds.
Hypothesis: "If the temperature of the hydrogen peroxide solution is increased, then the rate of catalase enzyme activity (measured by volume of O₂ produced per minute) will increase up to approximately 37°C, after which the rate will decrease significantly due to the irreversible denaturation of the catalase enzyme at higher temperatures."

🧪 Materials

  • Fresh potato (1 medium, peeled)
  • 3% Hydrogen peroxide solution (H₂O₂) — 400 mL
  • Water baths at 10, 20, 30, 37, 40, 50, 60, 70°C
  • Gas syringe (0–100 mL, precision ±0.5 mL)
  • Conical flask (100 mL) × 8
  • Rubber stopper with glass delivery tube
  • Electronic balance (±0.01 g)
  • Graduated cylinder (25 mL)
  • Thermometer (±0.5°C) — one per water bath
  • Digital stopwatch
  • Cork borer (6 mm diameter) and cutting board
  • Tweezers and spatula
  • Safety goggles and latex gloves
  • Ice bath and electric hotplate for temperature control
  • Paper towels and waste disposal container

📋 Method (Summary)

Step 1: Put on safety goggles and gloves. Using the cork borer, cut uniform potato cylinders from the same potato. Cut each cylinder into 5 mm discs. Weigh out 2.0 g of potato discs on the electronic balance for each trial. Set aside.
Step 2: Measure exactly 20 mL of 3% H₂O₂ solution into a clean 100 mL conical flask using the graduated cylinder.
Step 3: Place the flask into the water bath set to the first target temperature (10°C). Allow 5 minutes for the H₂O₂ to equilibrate. Use a thermometer to confirm the temperature before proceeding.
Step 4: Quickly add the 2.0 g of potato discs to the flask, immediately seal with the rubber stopper connected to the gas syringe, and start the stopwatch simultaneously.
Step 5: After exactly 60 seconds, record the volume of O₂ gas collected in the syringe (in mL) in the results table.
Step 6: Discard used potato discs and rinse the flask thoroughly. Repeat Steps 1–5 two more times at the same temperature to obtain three trials. Calculate the average O₂ volume.
Step 7: Repeat the entire process (Steps 1–6) for each of the remaining 7 temperatures: 20°C, 30°C, 37°C, 40°C, 50°C, 60°C, and 70°C.
Step 8: Record all data in the results table. Calculate the average for each temperature. Plot Graph 1 (O₂ volume vs temperature) and Graph 2 (Rate vs temperature). Analyse results and draw conclusions.

📌 Key observations from the graphs:
  • Enzyme activity rises steadily from 10°C to 37°C.
  • Activity peaks at 37°C with 35.0 mL O₂ produced — the optimum temperature.
  • A sharp decline occurs above 37°C as denaturation begins.
  • By 70°C, almost no activity remains (0.3 mL) — the enzyme is fully denatured.
  • The overall shape of Graph 1 is a bell curve (inverted U-shape).

⚗️ Reaction Summary

Reactants: Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) — catalysed by Catalase enzyme from potato cells
Products: Water (H₂O) + Oxygen gas (O₂)
2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ (gas)

The oxygen gas produced is what we measured using the gas syringe. More O₂ produced in 60 seconds = faster reaction rate = higher enzyme activity.
