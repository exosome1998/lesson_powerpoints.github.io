Most human cells contain DNA inside the nucleus. DNA stores biological instructions. Cells use these instructions to make proteins, which help build structures and carry out jobs.

Interesting fact4. DNA is extremely long

If the DNA from one human cell were stretched out, it would be about 2 metres long. It fits inside a tiny nucleus because it is tightly packed and coiled.
Why must DNA be tightly packed?

Read6. Human chromosomes

Most human body cells contain 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs. One chromosome in each pair usually comes from the biological mother and one from the biological father.
a. How many chromosomes are in most human body cells?
b. How many pairs is this?
c. Why are chromosomes arranged in pairs?

Interesting fact7. Not every human cell has a nucleus

Mature human red blood cells do not have a nucleus. Losing the nucleus creates more room to carry oxygen.
Why do mature red blood cells contain very little DNA?

Big idea10. All living things use DNA

Animals, plants, fungi and most microorganisms use DNA. The same four DNA bases—A, T, C and G—are used across life.
What four letters represent the DNA bases?
What does this suggest about life on Earth?

Interesting fact12. Bacteria organise DNA differently

Bacteria do not have a nucleus. Their main chromosome is usually circular and lies in the cell. Some bacteria also carry small DNA rings called plasmids.
Name one way bacterial DNA is different from human DNA.

Think13. Same DNA, different cells

A nerve cell and a skin cell usually contain the same DNA, but they use different genes. Different genes are switched on or off.
Why can two cells with the same DNA look and work differently?

Interesting fact14. Mitochondria have DNA

Mitochondria are structures that release energy for cells. They contain a small amount of their own DNA, separate from the DNA in the nucleus.
Where are the two locations of DNA in many human cells?

Read carefully15. Only a small part codes for proteins

Only about 1–2% of the human genome directly codes for proteins. Other DNA can help control when genes switch on, organise chromosomes, or perform other functions.
Why is it incorrect to call all non-protein-coding DNA “useless”?
