🔬 The Experiment — What Was Done

🎯
Aim
Find out how temperature affects how well the enzyme catalase works.
🧫
What Was Used
Potato discs (contain catalase) + hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) solution.
📏
What Was Measured
How much oxygen gas (O₂) was produced in 60 seconds.
🌡️
What Was Changed
The temperature — tested at 8 different temperatures (10°C to 70°C).
💡
The Hypothesis (prediction before the experiment)
"As temperature increases, enzyme activity will go up — until about 37°C. After that, the enzyme gets damaged by heat and stops working."

📘 Key Science — Read This First!

⚙️
What is an Enzyme?
A protein that speeds up chemical reactions in living things. It doesn't get used up — it can be reused over and over.
🔑
Lock & Key Model
Each enzyme has an active site — a specific shaped slot. Only the right molecule (substrate) can fit in, like a key in a lock.
🥔
Catalase (in potato)
The enzyme used in this experiment. It breaks down hydrogen peroxide (a toxic waste) into safe water + oxygen gas.
🔥
Denaturation
Too much heat permanently destroys the enzyme's shape. The active site breaks — the substrate can't fit anymore. This cannot be reversed.
⭐
Optimum Temperature
The best temperature for the enzyme. For catalase this is around 37°C — same as human body temperature.
🧊
Low Temperature
Cold = slow molecules = fewer collisions = slow reaction. The enzyme is fine, just sluggish. It is NOT damaged.

⚗️ The Chemical Reaction

Catalase breaks down hydrogen peroxide like this:

2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂ ↑

🧑‍🔬 How the Experiment Was Done

Cut 2.0 g of potato into small uniform discs using a cork borer.
Measure 20 mL of 3% hydrogen peroxide into a flask.
Put the flask in the water bath. Wait 5 minutes until it reaches the right temperature. Check with a thermometer.
Add the potato discs, seal the flask with a stopper connected to the gas syringe, and start the stopwatch immediately.
After exactly 60 seconds, record how much O₂ gas was collected (mL).
Repeat steps 1–5 two more times at the same temperature (3 trials total). Calculate the average.
Repeat everything for all 8 temperatures: 10, 20, 30, 37, 40, 50, 60, 70°C.
Record data in the table, plot the graphs, and analyse the results.
