📚   LESSON 1 — THE WEIGHT OF THE UNIVERSE
Learning Goal: Understand what forces are, how gravity works, and the difference between MASS and WEIGHT.
By the end of this lesson you should be able to explain why your weight would change on the Moon — but your mass would not!
1
What is a Force?

A force is a push or a pull on an object. You cannot see a force, but you can see what it does — it can make things speed up, slow down, change direction or change shape.

Key fact: Forces are measured in NEWTONS (N), named after the scientist Sir Isaac Newton. The symbol for Newtons is a capital N.  Example: "The force is 10 N."

Forces come in two types:

CONTACT FORCES NON-CONTACT FORCES

Need to TOUCH the object to work.

Examples: friction, push, pull, air resistance.

Work WITHOUT touching the object.

Examples: gravity, magnetism, static electricity.

2
Non-Contact Force: Gravity

Gravity is a non-contact force that pulls every object with mass towards every other object with mass. The bigger the mass of an object, the stronger its gravitational pull.

Think of it this way: the Earth is enormous, so it has a very strong gravitational pull.
This pull is what keeps you on the ground, keeps the Moon orbiting Earth, and keeps
Earth orbiting the Sun.

On Earth, gravity pulls downward with a strength of approximately 10 Newtons for every kilogram of mass. Scientists write this as:

Earth's gravitational field strength (g) =  10 N/kg

3
What is Mass?
MASSUnit: kilograms (kg)
Definition: The amount of matter (stuff) that makes up an object. A bowling ball has more mass than a tennis ball because it contains more matter.
Example: A schoolbag full of books might have a mass of 4 kg. An empty schoolbag might have a mass of 0.5 kg.
CRITICAL FACT: Mass NEVER changes, no matter where you are in the universe.
If you travel to the Moon or float in deep space, your mass stays exactly the same.
Mass does NOT depend on gravity — it is just the amount of stuff inside an object.

We measure mass using a digital scale or a triple beam balance. These tools measure how much matter is in an object, not how hard gravity pulls on it.

👆 click to reveal model answer
✓ Model Answer
The astronaut's mass is still 75 kg on the Moon.
Mass measures the amount of matter in an object. Matter does not disappear when location changes. Mass is independent of gravity — it is the same everywhere in the universe.
4
What is Weight?
WEIGHTUnit: Newtons (N)
Definition: The force of gravity pulling down on an object's mass. Weight is a FORCE, so it is measured in Newtons — not kilograms!
Example: A person with a mass of 60 kg has a weight of 600 N on Earth (because g = 10 N/kg).
CRITICAL FACT: Weight DOES change depending on where you are in the universe.
If you go to the Moon (where gravity is weaker), your weight will be less.
In deep space (almost zero gravity), you would be nearly weightless — but your mass is still the same!

We measure weight using a spring balance (also called a Newton meter). The spring stretches further when a heavier object is attached, and the scale shows the force in Newtons.

5
The Formula — Calculating Weight

We can calculate weight using a simple formula:

Weight  =  Mass  ×  Gravity
W  =  m  ×  g
(Newtons)      (kilograms)      (N/kg)
🔍 WORKED EXAMPLE 1 — Weight on Earth

Question: What is the weight of a 5 kg bag of potatoes on Earth? (g = 10 N/kg)

Step 1 — Write the formula:   W = m × g

Step 2 — Substitute values:   W = 5 × 10

Step 3 — Calculate:           W = 50 N


Answer: The bag of potatoes has a weight of 50 N on Earth.

🔍 WORKED EXAMPLE 2 — Weight on the Moon

Question: The same 5 kg bag of potatoes is taken to the Moon. What is its weight? (g = 1.6 N/kg)

Step 1 — Write the formula:   W = m × g

Step 2 — Substitute values:   W = 5 × 1.6

Step 3 — Calculate:           W = 8 N


Answer: The bag of potatoes has a weight of only 8 N on the Moon — much lighter! But its MASS is still 5 kg.

Validity — Was It a Fair Test?
VALIDITY means: the experiment actually tested what you intended to test. For an experiment to be VALID, only the independent variable (IV) should change. Everything else — the controlled variables (CVs) — must stay the same. If something else changed accidentally (e.g. you used a different spring balance halfway through), your experiment would NOT be valid because you wouldn't know what caused the difference in results.

Write Your Conclusion
A conclusion is a short paragraph that directly answers your INQUIRY QUESTION. A good conclusion includes ALL the following:
   ① Answers the inquiry question directly
   ② States whether the hypothesis was supported or refuted
   ③ Refers to specific data (numbers) as evidence
   ④ Mentions the mathematical relationship (W = m × g)
   ⑤ Is written in past tense
Sentence starters to guide you:

② This result supported / refuted my hypothesis, which predicted that...

③ For example, when the mass was _____ kg, the measured weight was _____ N.

④ This is consistent with the formula W = m × g, because...

⑤ In conclusion, weight is directly proportional to mass, meaning...

This investigation found that as the mass of an object increased, the weight acting on it also increased proportionally. This is consistent with the formula W = m × g, because with g held constant at 10 N/kg, weight scales linearly with mass. In conclusion, weight is directly proportional to mass: doubling the mass doubles the weight.
