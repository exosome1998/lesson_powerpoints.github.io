🧩 1. What is a Polyatomic Ion?

The prefix "Poly" means many. A polyatomic ion is a group of atoms bonded together that act as a single unit with one overall charge.

The Lego Analogy: Imagine super-gluing a few small Lego bricks together. They form a unique new shape that can't be broken apart. When you build with it, it moves as one solid piece.

⚠️ Stop! You can NEVER change the small numbers inside a polyatomic ion. For example, the '4' in Sulfate (SO₄) must always remain a 4!
🛡️ 2. The Bracket Rule

When using the criss-cross method, if you need more than one of a polyatomic ion, you MUST wrap it in brackets before adding the small number outside.

Mg²⁺ and NO₃⁻  →  Mg(NO₃)₂

If you only need one, do not use brackets! (e.g., Na⁺ and NO₃⁻ is just NaNO₃).

💧 3. Aqueous (aq) Solutions

When solid ionic compounds dissolve in water, they break apart into their free-floating ions. We use the state symbol (aq) to show this.

Remember: Polyatomic ions do not break apart in water! A nitrate ion (NO₃⁻) stays as a single NO₃⁻ chunk floating around.

⚙️ 5. Step-By-Step Criss-Cross

Example A: Calcium nitrate

  • Cation: Calcium is Ca²⁺ | Anion: Nitrate is NO₃⁻
  • Cross Over: The '2' drops to the outside of NO₃. Because it's polyatomic, add brackets.
  • Formula: Ca(NO₃)₂

Example B: Ammonium sulfide

  • Cation: Ammonium is NH₄⁺ | Anion: Sulfide is S²⁻
  • Cross Over: The '2' drops to the outside of NH₄. We need brackets around NH₄. The '1' vanishes.
  • Formula: (NH₄)₂S

Example C: Aluminum phosphate

  • Cation: Aluminum is Al³⁺ | Anion: Phosphate is PO₄³⁻
  • Cross Over: The charges balance out perfectly at 1:1, so no sub-numbers are dropped. No brackets!
  • Formula: AlPO₄

Example D: Iron(III) sulfate

  • Cation: Iron(III) is Fe³⁺ | Anion: Sulfate is SO₄²⁻
  • Cross Over: The '2' drops to Fe. The '3' drops outside SO₄. Use brackets!
  • Formula: Fe₂(SO₄)₃
🧪 6. Dissolving & Dissociation

When solid ionic compounds dissolve in water, they separate (dissociate) into their independent positive and negative ions. We write the solid on the left and the free-floating aqueous ions on the right.

Translating these is just breaking them down into their original ions:

Ex 1: Solid sodium nitrate dissolves into aqueous sodium ions and aqueous nitrate ions.

  • Solid Sodium nitrate: NaNO₃ (s)
  • Aqueous Sodium ion: Na⁺ (aq)
  • Aqueous Nitrate ion: NO₃⁻ (aq)
NaNO₃ (s) → Na⁺ (aq) + NO₃⁻ (aq)

Ex 2: Solid potassium hydroxide dissolves into aqueous potassium ions and aqueous hydroxide ions.

  • Solid Potassium hydroxide: KOH (s)
  • Aqueous Potassium ion: K⁺ (aq)
  • Aqueous Hydroxide ion: OH⁻ (aq)
KOH (s) → K⁺ (aq) + OH⁻ (aq)

Ex 3: Solid ammonium chloride dissolves into aqueous ammonium ions and aqueous chloride ions.

  • Solid Ammonium chloride: NH₄Cl (s)
  • Aqueous Ammonium ion: NH₄⁺ (aq)
  • Aqueous Chloride ion: Cl⁻ (aq)
NH₄Cl (s) → NH₄⁺ (aq) + Cl⁻ (aq)

Ex 4: Solid magnesium sulfate dissolves into aqueous magnesium ions and aqueous sulfate ions.

  • Solid Magnesium sulfate: MgSO₄ (s)
  • Aqueous Magnesium ion: Mg²⁺ (aq)
  • Aqueous Sulfate ion: SO₄²⁻ (aq)
MgSO₄ (s) → Mg²⁺ (aq) + SO₄²⁻ (aq)

C — Find the Error10 marks

Identify the critical mistake in these student formulas and correct them.

1. "Barium hydroxide" → BaOH₂

Missing brackets around OH. Should be Ba(OH)₂.

2. "Sodium carbonate" → Na(CO₃)₂

Na is 1+ and CO₃ is 2-. Should be Na₂CO₃ (no brackets needed).

3. "Calcium nitrate" → Ca₂NO₃

Ca is 2+ and NO₃ is 1-. Crossed over incorrectly. Should be Ca(NO₃)₂.

4. "Iron(III) hydroxide" → FeOH₃

Missing brackets around the polyatomic ion OH. Should be Fe(OH)₃.

5. "Potassium nitrate" → (K)NO₃

No brackets are needed when there is only one metal atom. Just KNO₃.
