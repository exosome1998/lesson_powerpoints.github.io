Diagram Analysis: Short Answer Review (Click to Reveal)

1. (Pane A1) Why does the diagram connect a telescope looking at the sun, ocean waves, and the equation E=mc²?

It illustrates that whether it's radiant energy (sun), kinetic energy (waves), or matter itself (E=mc²), energy exists in wildly different, but interconnected, forms across the universe.

2. (Pane A2) According to the visual, what are two simple physical actions a person can take to conserve energy?

The person in the diagram is shown manually unplugging a cord from the wall to stop "vampire power" and adjusting a programmable wall thermostat.

3. (Pane A3) Looking at the TE = PE + KE graph alongside the pendulum, what happens to the blue PE line when the red KE line reaches its peak?

The blue Potential Energy line drops to its lowest point (zero) exactly when the Kinetic Energy hits its peak, proving that one converts directly into the other to keep Total Energy (TE) constant.

4. (Pane B1) Compare the source of kinetic energy shown in the moving train versus the running soccer player.

The train gets its massive kinetic energy primarily from electrical or chemical (fuel) input driving its heavy mass, whereas the soccer player uses biological chemical energy (from food) to create motion.

5. (Pane B2) How do the water tower and the compressed spring visually represent the two main types of potential energy?

The water tower represents "Gravitational Potential Energy" (stored via height/position), while the coiled spring represents "Elastic Potential Energy" (stored via physical deformation/stretching).

6. (Pane B3) In the hydroelectric dam diagram, at what specific physical point does the stored Potential Energy actively become Kinetic Energy?

The conversion happens the moment the water enters the intake pipe and begins flowing downward through the penstock toward the turbine, changing from stored water to moving water.

7. (Pane C1) Based on the efficiency diagram, why is the character looking shocked at the old incandescent bulb's statistics?

Because the diagram shows that 90% of the energy going into the old bulb is entirely wasted as heat, producing only 10% useful light output compared to the LED.

8. (Pane C2) In the diagram showing the cross-section of a wire, what do the moving blue particles represent compared to the stationary red ones?

The moving blue particles with minus signs represent negatively charged electrons flowing to create a current, while the red particles represent the positively charged protons locked within the atoms of the conductive wire.

9. (Pane D1) Why is the water pipe shown with a single, continuous flow next to the series circuit of three bulbs?

It visually reinforces that in a series circuit, the current (water flow) has only one path to take, meaning the exact same flow must pass through every single component sequentially.

10. (Pane C3) How does the chalkboard equation V = I * R relate to the battery, wire, and resistor drawing next to it?

The battery provides the Voltage (V, the pressure), the wire carries the Current (I, the flow), and the resistor creates the Resistance (R, the pinch), demonstrating how the three variables mathematically interact.

11. (Pane D2) Visually, what happens to the red arrows (representing current) when they hit the junctions in the parallel circuit diagram?

The arrows split and take multiple different branches simultaneously, visually demonstrating that total current divides among the available paths, unlike in a series circuit.

12. (Pane D3) Looking at the cross-section of the house, why are the different rooms lit independently, and what type of circuit makes this possible?

The house uses parallel wiring. This allows the electricity to flow independently to the upstairs and downstairs rooms, meaning turning off the lights in one room doesn't cut power to the others.
