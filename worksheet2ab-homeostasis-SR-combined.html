L1 — Negative Feedback Loops★☆☆

1. Negative feedback is when the body detects a change and reverses it.

2. This loop is how the body maintains homeostasis.

3. If you get too hot, negative feedback makes you cool down.

4. The ideal, normal level the body wants to stay at is called the set point.

5. If blood pressure drops, the response will make it go up.

6. Without negative feedback, we would quickly become sick/dead.

L2 — Blood Sugar Feedback Loop★★☆

1. High blood sugar is the stimulus for the pancreas.

2. The pancreas responds by releasing insulin.

3. Insulin forces cells to absorb sugar, which lowers the blood sugar level.

4. This creates a perfect negative feedback loop.

5. If sugar gets too low, the pancreas releases glucagon instead.

6. Glucagon acts to bring blood sugar back up to the set point.

5. By 90 minutes, the body successfully restored homeostasis.

6. The green dashed line represents the healthy set point.

L4 — Numeracy: Urine Output📊

1. Fluid filtered minus fluid reabsorbed equals urine made. Urine = 0.1 L.

2. Convert the urine output into millilitres (1 L = 1000 mL): 100 mL.

3. If the patient runs a marathon and sweats heavily, ADH hormone will increase.

4. Higher ADH means the kidneys will reabsorb more water.

5. If reabsorption rises to 7.45 L, the new urine output is 0.05 L.

6. This negative feedback loop protects against dehydration.

L5 — Fever: Moving the Set Point★★☆

1. Normally, human core temperature has a set point of 37 °C.

2. During an infection, the brain increases this set point.

3. Because the new target is higher, you feel cold and start shivering.

4. Shivering (the effector) generates heat to reach the new set point.

5. A fever is a protective response designed to kill bacteria/viruses.

6. When the fever breaks, negative feedback forces you to sweat to cool down.

L6 — Blood Pressure & Shock★★☆

1. Sudden blood loss causes blood pressure to drop (the stimulus).

2. Pressure receptors detect this and alert the brain.

3. The brain responds by making blood vessels constrict/narrow.

4. This negative feedback loop makes the pressure go back up.

5. The brain also tells the heart to beat faster.

6. Failure of this negative feedback loop results in clinical shock.

L7 — Graph Analysis: Temperature📊

1. The patient's normal set point is 37 °C.

2. What is the lowest temperature reached? 35 °C.

3. Calculate the temp drop between 0 and 10 minutes: 2 °C.

4. Calculate the rate of warming from 10m to 30m: 0.1 °C per minute.

5. What bodily response (effector) caused the line to rise? Shivering

6. The recovery from 35°C to 37°C is proof of negative feedback.

L8 — Concussion Testing★★☆

1. Shining a light in the eye provides a stimulus.

2. The retina acts as the receptor.

3. The negative feedback response protects the eye by making the pupil smaller.

4. The effector performing this action is the iris muscle.

5. If the pupil doesn't react, it implies the brain is damaged.

6. This reflex proves the sensory and motor nerves are still working.

L9 — Numeracy: Heart Rate (ECG)📊

1. The graph shows 3 beats in 1 second. How many in 60 secs? 180

2. Normal resting heart rate is between 60 and 100 BPM.

3. This patient has a dangerously high heart rate (tachycardia).

4. Tachycardia could be a response to the stimulus of extreme fear/exercise.

5. The effector creating these beats is the heart muscle.

6. A high heart rate is driven by the hormone adrenaline.

L10 — Anesthesia & Nerve Blocks★★☆

1. A scalpel cutting skin acts as an extreme stimulus.

2. Pain receptors physically detect this cut.

3. However, anesthesia chemically blocks the sensory nerves.

4. Because the signal is blocked, it never reaches the coordinator/brain.

5. If the brain receives no alert, there is no pain response.

6. Paralytics work similarly but block motor nerves to muscles.

Choose a disease/medical scenario. Map the S-R pathway:

Scenario: e.g. Asthma attack

What is the internal stimulus? High blood CO2

What is the effector? Diaphragm / lung muscles

What is the response? Breathe faster / hyperventilate

How does this act as Negative Feedback? Removes the excess CO2

L12 — Medical Vocabulary★☆☆

1. Homeostasis: maintaining a stable internal environment.

2. Hypoglycemia: dangerously low blood sugar.

3. Hyperthermia: dangerously high body temperature.

4. Vasodilation: blood vessels get wider to lose heat.

5. Vasoconstriction: blood vessels get narrow to save heat.

6. Pathogen: a bacteria/virus that acts as a stimulus for fever.

L13 — Master Graph Analysis: Type 1 Diabetes★★★

1. Both patients eat a meal at 0 hr. Blood glucose initially goes up for both.

2. Patient A's glucose peaks at 1 hour and returns to 90 mg/dL by 2 hours.

3. Patient A has a functioning negative feedback loop.

4. Which patient is suffering from Type 1 Diabetes? Patient B.

5. Calculate the difference in blood sugar between Patient A and B at 2 hours: 160 mg/dL.

6. Patient B's graph stays high because their body cannot produce insulin.

L14 — Master: Broken Loops★★★

1. In allergies, a harmless food acts as an extreme stimulus.

2. The body's natural response is dangerous; airways swell/close.

3. An EpiPen injects artificial adrenaline (hormone) to fix this.

4. The medicine forces airway muscles to relax/open.

5. Medicine is used to manually restore homeostasis when a loop breaks.
