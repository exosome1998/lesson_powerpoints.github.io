🟢 BACTERIA: The Black Death

In the 1300s, a horrifying pandemic swept across Europe and Asia, killing an estimated 50 million people (one-third of the population). To stop it, coastal cities forced arriving ships to wait 40 days before unloading, creating the first ever quarantine.

Transmission: Spread primarily by fleas living on black rats. The fleas acted as a vector, injecting bacteria directly into the blood.

Damage: Bacteria multiplied in lymph nodes, causing massive painful swellings called buboes. It released powerful toxins causing tissue to die and turn black.

🩺 Treatment: Today, if a modern human catches the plague, it is easily cured using antibiotics.
💧 BACTERIA: Cholera (1854)

In 1854, a deadly Cholera outbreak hit London. At the time, doctors believed diseases were caused by "Miasma" (bad air).

Dr. John Snow didn't have a microscope to see the tiny bacteria. Instead, he drew a map of all the deaths and realized they clustered around a single water pump on Broad Street.

Transmission: Cholera spreads via the Vehicle route (drinking contaminated water). Removing the pump handle ended the outbreak! This map helped prove Miasma wrong and supported Germ Theory.

🔴 VIRUS: The 1918 Spanish Flu

As World War I ended, a deadly strain of the influenza virus swept the globe, killing over 50 million. It was a massive pandemic.

The Pathogen: The H1N1 Virus. Viruses are non-living and must enter a host cell to reproduce.

Transmission: It spread through indirect contact. When an infected soldier sneezed, airborne droplets floated into nearby lungs.

Why was it so deadly? It triggered an extreme "cytokine storm"—an overreaction from the body's own immune system. Massive inflammation caused lungs to fill with fluid.

💉 Prevention: We cannot cure viruses with antibiotics. We rely on annual vaccines to create memory cells and achieve immunity.
🦠 VIRUS: Smallpox Eradication

Smallpox was a horrifying virus causing highly contagious blisters. In 1796, Dr. Edward Jenner noticed that milkmaids who caught a mild virus called Cowpox never caught Smallpox. He used this discovery to invent the world's first vaccine!

Thanks to a global vaccination campaign centuries later, Smallpox became the first human disease completely eradicated from Earth in 1980.

🟤 FUNGUS: Candida auris

Fungi normally cause mild issues (Athlete's Foot). But a new fungus, C. auris, emerged in 2009. It spreads rapidly in hospitals.

The Threat: Fungi reproduce via tiny spores. Due to the overuse of anti-fungal chemicals in farming, it has mutated into a dangerous superbug that resists modern medication.

🥩 PRION: Mad Cow Disease

Not all pathogens are germs! A Prion is a misfolded protein in the brain. It contains no DNA. It spreads when humans or animals eat infected brain or nervous tissue. Because it isn't alive, you cannot kill a prion by cooking the meat! It causes fatal brain damage.

🔵 PARASITE: Malaria

Unlike a global pandemic, Malaria is Endemic. It is constantly present in specific tropical regions. It kills over 600,000 annually.

The Pathogen: A single-celled parasite (Plasmodium). Parasites must live inside a host to steal nutrients.

Transmission: It relies on the Anopheles mosquito. When a mosquito bites an infected person, it sucks up the parasite. When it bites the next person, it injects the germ. The mosquito is a living vector.

Damage: It invades and destroys Red Blood Cells causing cyclical fevers.

🛡️ Prevention: Because it relies on a flying bug, using insecticide-treated nets over beds at night effectively blocks transmission.
🦇 Zoonotic Diseases

A Zoonotic disease successfully jumps from an animal to a human. For example, the Rabies virus jumps via a dog bite. As humans destroy forests (deforestation), we come into closer contact with wild animals, making zoonotic outbreaks more common.

🕵️ Epidemiology & The R-Naught

Epidemiologists (disease detectives) use contact tracing to figure out who infected who. They hunt for Patient Zero (the index case) to locate the source.

They also calculate a germ's R0 (R-Naught) value. This tells us how contagious it is. If a virus has an R0 of 3, one sick person will infect three others on average.

D — Transmission Analysis8 marks

1. The Black Death and Malaria both rely on a "Vector". Explain what a Vector is, and identify the specific vector for each disease.

A vector is a living organism that carries/injects a germ.
Black Death Vector = Fleas (on rats).
Malaria Vector = Anopheles Mosquito.

2. Unlike those two, how did the 1918 Flu spread?

Indirectly via airborne droplets when someone sneezed/coughed.

3. Why do Fungi like C. auris thrive in hospitals?

Hospitals are warm, and spores can survive for a long time
on medical equipment (fomites).
E — Epidemiological Logic8 marks

1. Dr. John Snow didn't have a microscope to see the Cholera bacteria. How did he logically prove that "bad air" wasn't causing the disease?

He mapped the deaths and saw they clustered around one
specific water pump, proving it was a waterborne vehicle.

2. Why do modern epidemiologists want to find "Patient Zero" during a new viral outbreak?

To understand where the disease came from (e.g. zoonotic jump)
and to trace everyone they contacted to stop the spread.

3. Disease A has an R0 of 2. Disease B has an R0 of 15. Which is more likely to cause a pandemic and why?

Disease B, because it is much more contagious (1 person
infects 15 others, causing rapid exponential spread).
F — Superbug Threat4 marks

1. What does it mean when a pathogen is labeled a "Superbug"?

It means the pathogen has mutated and is highly resistant
to standard medications (it can't be killed easily).
